METHOD · Risk index = 35 % flow magnitude + 30 % 7-day rate of rise + 35 % forecast outlook. Flow magnitude and forecast outlook are ranked as percentiles against a 10-year same-season GloFAS climatology at the same grid cell (±15 days of year, current event excluded) — so "high" means high *for this reach at this time of year*, not merely high within the last 90 days. If the 10-year history can't be fetched, the index falls back to the previous 90-day-peak scaling and says so in the readout. In the braided Jamuna and lower Meghna, severe bankline retreat also depends on local bank material, char dynamics and channel shifting that no discharge index captures — treat this as a screening tool, and verify banklines with the imagery fade.

METHOD · Silt transport index = (Q/Q̄)^1.6 × 100 — suspended-sediment transport capacity rises roughly as discharge to the 1.5–2 power, so the index expresses each day's silt-moving power relative to the reach's own 3-year mean; cumulative curves compare whole years. It is a *capacity* proxy: actual loads also depend on upstream supply, and absolute tonnage is not claimed. Land-change computation classifies MODIS 7-2-1 pixels (250 m) on the chosen day of two years: water→land = alluvion, land→water = diluvion; clouds read as land and high water hides low chars, so compare dry-season dates (Jan–Mar) and treat areas as indicative. Official land records (diara/char surveys) and BWDB/CEGIS studies govern real alluvion–diluvion determinations.

Numerical models Muskingum routing · linear-reservoir aquifer
Muskingum routing (Cunge, 1969; Chow et al., 1988): the upstream hydrograph is translated and attenuated through a conceptual channel storage; fitting K and X to the GloFAS record reveals the flood-wave travel time between your two reaches.
Linear-reservoir aquifer model (Chow et al., 1988): daily recharge (P−ET₀) fills a store that drains in proportion to its content, S′ = R − S/k. Fitting the time constant k to ERA5 deep soil moisture measures this reach's groundwater memory — how long the monsoon lives in the subsurface.
METHOD · This station computes cross-station analytics from the same free feeds the other stations use, joined at one coordinate. (1) Falling-limb bank-failure index = river fall rate (7-day discharge drop, 25 %/week saturates) × deep-soil saturation percentile, scaled by local coupling strength — a screening implementation of the pore-pressure mechanism by which saturated banks collapse after flood peaks; it is not a geotechnical stability analysis, and ERA5 floodplain soil moisture stands in for a bank piezometer. (2) Recharge lag analyzer = Pearson correlation between discharge and deep soil moisture with the river series shifted 0–60 days; the peak's position estimates how long monsoon water takes to reach the subsurface here, and its height (r) how hydraulically connected river and floodplain aquifer are. (3) Scorecard = per-calendar-year anomalies of monsoon flow, annual silt-transport work, monsoon P−ET₀ and the post-monsoon groundwater crest, for spotting drought triple-signatures and flood-heavy years. Correlation is not causation: irrigation pumping, rainfall driving both series, and model error all share these signals. (4) Numerical models: Muskingum channel routing (Cunge, 1969; Chow, Maidment and Mays, 1988) fitted by grid search with the Nash–Sutcliffe efficiency (Nash and Sutcliffe, 1970), and a single linear-reservoir groundwater store (Chow et al., 1988) fitted to ERA5 deep soil moisture — the two simplest academically standard representations of channel coupling and aquifer memory, chosen for transparency over sophistication. (5) Whole-system interaction chain = best-lag Pearson correlations between z-scored 3-year daily series along the corridor the selected reach belongs to (Brahmaputra, Ganges or Meghna): headwater warmth → upstream discharge (0–45 d window, the melt signal), upstream → this reach (0–20 d, flood routing), this reach → the estuary mouth (0–15 d, delivery to the Coastal station), this reach → deep soil moisture (from the recharge lag analyzer), plus a dilution read tying the reach's current seasonal flow percentile to the Effluent station's loading register. Lags pinned at a window edge mean the true lag may lie beyond the searched range; monsoon seasonality drives all series simultaneously and inflates every r, so read the lags comparatively, not as proof of causation.

    PURPOSE · The Bridge station is the Observatory's interface to Bangladesh's water institutions. BWDB (gauges, groundwater wells, morphology surveys), WARPO (the National Water Resources Database), CEGIS (annual erosion predictions and geospatial studies), IWM (calibrated hydraulic and hydrodynamic model results) and BIWTA (hydrographic charts and navigation routes) hold the authoritative ground truth that satellites and global models approximate. This station lets authorized users bring that data in — as CSV series or GeoJSON layers — overlay it on the same maps, and quantify the agreement between official observations and the free global feeds the other stations run on. Everything runs client-side: institutional data never leaves the user's machine, which keeps data-sharing agreements intact.

    METHOD · Two complementary halves, mirroring how pollution is actually monitored. (1) Optical proxies: Sentinel-2/HLS and true/false-colour composites from NASA GIBS reveal turbidity and suspended-sediment plumes, algal blooms (eutrophication from nutrient loading) and the spatial footprint of discharges; these are physical proxies — remote sensing cannot measure chromium, BOD, ammonia or other chemicals directly, and optically dark "clean-looking" water can still be highly polluted. (2) Loading register: a faithful re-implementation of the US EPA Water Pollutant Loading Tool (DMR) method — load = concentration × wastewater flow × duration × unit conversion (EPA, 2024) — ranked by facility and pollutant, with a toxic-weighting option and a load-over-permit-limit flag. (3) Facility results: a facility-level rollup of the register, patterned on EPA ECHO's Water Pollution Search "View Facilities" results table — sortable, filterable, downloadable, and drillable into a per-facility pollutant report. It requires reported or measured effluent data as input (entered, pasted or CSV); it does not invent concentrations. In Bangladesh the authoritative effluent and ambient water-quality data rest with the Department of Environment (DoE) under the ECR rules, with BWDB, and with academic monitoring; use this register to organize and rank that data, and the proxies to target where new sampling is most needed. Screening tool — not a compliance determination.

    METHOD · The compound-flood screen combines three 0–100 sub-scores at the chosen point: wave energy (significant wave height scaled against a 3 m open-water reference — a fixed threshold, since long marine climatologies aren't available from this feed), river discharge (percentile rank against a 10-year same-season GloFAS climatology at the same cell, ±15 days of year, current event excluded; falls back to the 90-day maximum if history can't be fetched — the tag under the discharge number says which was used), and an active-cyclone proximity flag (any Bay of Bengal track point within ~150 km and 3 days of today, from the list above).     Screening indicator only, built from surface weather — not a snowpack or streamflow model. A rise here is a plausible early signal that Ganges/Brahmaputra flow downstream may increase over the following 1–3 weeks, not a forecast of it.
    DATA · Imagery: NASA GIBS — MODIS/Terra NDSI Snow Cover (250 m, daily), with VIIRS true color, MODIS true color and Harmonized Landsat–Sentinel as fallbacks over persistent cloud. Point weather: ERA5-Land reanalysis via the free Open-Meteo Historical Weather API — daily mean/max/min temperature, snowfall sum, precipitation sum; global coverage, no key, roughly a week of latency. Sites are illustrative headwater points along the Brahmaputra, Ganges, Teesta and Barak/Meghna catchments — not gauged monitoring stations.
    METHOD · The melt-season index averages two 0–100 sub-scores: above-freezing warmth and snowfall decline. Warmth is the current 7-day mean temperature's percentile rank against a 10-year same-season ERA5-Land record at the same point (±15 days of year, current period excluded), gated to zero when the mean is below 0 °C since melt needs above-freezing air; if the 10-year history can't be fetched it falls back to a fixed 15 °C scaling, and the tag under the temperature says which was used. Snowfall decline is the drop from the prior 30-day snowfall total to the most recent 30-day total, as a fraction of the prior total. Both are proxies for melt becoming active, not measurements of actual snowpack loss or meltwater volume — satellites don't measure snow depth directly at this resolution, and reanalysis temperature is a modeled estimate, not a station reading. This station exists to connect a plausible upstream cause to what the Riverbank station observes downstream 1–3 weeks later; it is not a hydrological forecast.
