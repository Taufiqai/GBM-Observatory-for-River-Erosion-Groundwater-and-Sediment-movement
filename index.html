Point data: ERA5/ERA5-Land reanalysis via the free Open-Meteo archive API — daily precipitation, FAO reference evapotranspiration (ET₀) and layered soil moisture (~9–11 km grid, ~5–6 day latency); 16-day rainfall outlook from the Open-Meteo forecast API. No API keys required.
METHOD · Stress index = 40 % subsurface dryness (today's 1–2.6 m soil wetness ranked against the same season in the 3-year record) + 35 % recharge deficit (this 90-day P−ET₀ vs the same window in prior years) + 25 % dry outlook (16-day forecast rain vs forecast ET₀). Deep soil moisture is a *proxy* for shallow water-table behaviour in the Bengal alluvium — it cannot see pumping-driven drawdown in deep aquifers (Dhaka, Barind). To partly close that gap, the long-term depletion signal panel fits a linear trend to 10–11 years of annual water balance (ΣP − ΣET₀ per year): the seasonal index mean-reverts every monsoon and looks normal even during multi-year drawdown, whereas a persistently negative annual-balance trend is the climatic depletion pressure. Where wells fall but this trend is flat, the loss is abstraction rather than rainfall — confirm with GRACE-FO and BWDB/CGWB hydrographs. Values are model/reanalysis output, not well measurements. Screening tool only.

METHOD · Silt transport index = (Q/Q̄)^1.6 × 100 — suspended-sediment transport capacity rises roughly as discharge to the 1.5–2 power, so the index expresses each day's silt-moving power relative to the reach's own 3-year mean; cumulative curves compare whole years. It is a *capacity* proxy: actual loads also depend on upstream supply, and absolute tonnage is not claimed. Land-change computation classifies MODIS 7-2-1 pixels (250 m) on the chosen day of two years: water→land = alluvion, land→water = diluvion; clouds read as land and high water hides low chars, so compare dry-season dates (Jan–Mar) and treat areas as indicative. The channel-migration figure scans each image row for the wetted-channel centroid in both years and reports the lateral shift in km (mean, peak, and m/yr) plus net channel widening/narrowing — a direct planform measurement of the braided/char dynamics that a discharge index cannot capture, though still bounded by the 250 m pixel and dry-season classification caveats. Official land records (diara/char surveys) and BWDB/CEGIS studies govern real alluvion–diluvion determinations, and this remains a macro-screening tool, not a geotechnical bank-stability model.

Numerical models Muskingum routing · linear-reservoir aquifer
Muskingum routing (Cunge, 1969; Chow et al., 1988): the upstream hydrograph is translated and attenuated through a conceptual channel storage; fitting K and X to the GloFAS record reveals the flood-wave travel time between your two reaches.
Linear-reservoir aquifer model (Chow et al., 1988): daily recharge (P−ET₀) fills a store that drains in proportion to its content, S′ = R − S/k. Fitting the time constant k to ERA5 deep soil moisture measures this reach's groundwater memory — how long the monsoon lives in the subsurface.
METHOD · This station computes cross-station analytics from the same free feeds the other stations use, joined at one coordinate. (1) Falling-limb bank-failure index = river fall rate (7-day discharge drop, 25 %/week saturates) × deep-soil saturation percentile, scaled by local coupling strength — a screening implementation of the pore-pressure mechanism by which saturated banks collapse after flood peaks; it is not a geotechnical stability analysis, and ERA5 floodplain soil moisture stands in for a bank piezometer. (2) Recharge lag analyzer = Pearson correlation between discharge and deep soil moisture with the river series shifted 0–60 days; the peak's position estimates how long monsoon water takes to reach the subsurface here, and its height (r) how hydraulically connected river and floodplain aquifer are. (3) Scorecard = per-calendar-year anomalies of monsoon flow, annual silt-transport work, monsoon P−ET₀ and the post-monsoon groundwater crest, for spotting drought triple-signatures and flood-heavy years. Correlation is not causation: irrigation pumping, rainfall driving both series, and model error all share these signals. (4) Numerical models: Muskingum channel routing (Cunge, 1969; Chow, Maidment and Mays, 1988) fitted by grid search with the Nash–Sutcliffe efficiency (Nash and Sutcliffe, 1970), and a single linear-reservoir groundwater store (Chow et al., 1988) fitted to ERA5 deep soil moisture — the two simplest academically standard representations of channel coupling and aquifer memory, chosen for transparency over sophistication.

    PURPOSE · The Bridge station is the Observatory's interface to Bangladesh's water institutions. BWDB (gauges, groundwater wells, morphology surveys), WARPO (the National Water Resources Database), CEGIS (annual erosion predictions and geospatial studies), IWM (calibrated hydraulic and hydrodynamic model results) and BIWTA (hydrographic charts and navigation routes) hold the authoritative ground truth that satellites and global models approximate. This station lets authorized users bring that data in — as CSV series or GeoJSON layers — overlay it on the same maps, and quantify the agreement between official observations and the free global feeds the other stations run on. Everything runs client-side: institutional data never leaves the user's machine, which keeps data-sharing agreements intact.

    Load (kg/yr) = concentration (mg/L) × flow (m³/day) × days ÷ 1000. Toxic-weighted load applies a relative toxicity factor so a kilo of chromium ranks above a kilo of BOD. ▲ marks loads exceeding the entered permit limit. Click a column header to sort.
    METHOD · Two complementary halves, mirroring how pollution is actually monitored. (1) Optical proxies: Sentinel-2/HLS and true/false-colour composites from NASA GIBS reveal turbidity and suspended-sediment plumes, algal blooms (eutrophication from nutrient loading) and the spatial footprint of discharges; these are physical proxies — remote sensing cannot measure chromium, BOD, ammonia or other chemicals directly, and optically dark "clean-looking" water can still be highly polluted. (2) Loading register: a faithful re-implementation of the US EPA Water Pollutant Loading Tool (DMR) method — load = concentration × wastewater flow × duration × unit conversion (EPA, 2024) — ranked by facility and pollutant, with a toxic-weighting option and a load-over-permit-limit flag. It requires reported or measured effluent data as input (entered, pasted or CSV); it does not invent concentrations. In Bangladesh the authoritative effluent and ambient water-quality data rest with the Department of Environment (DoE) under the ECR rules, with BWDB, and with academic monitoring; use this register to organize and rank that data, and the proxies to target where new sampling is most needed. Screening tool — not a compliance determination.
